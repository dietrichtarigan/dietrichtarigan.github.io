A Resurgence in Nuclear Energy

The global nuclear industry is undergoing a profound transformation. Driven by the critical needs for decarbonization and enhanced energy security, nuclear power is experiencing a renaissance. This dashboard provides an interactive guide to the complex ecosystem of companies, technologies, and processes that define this vital sector.

The Two Lifecycles of Nuclear Power

To understand the industry, we must look at two interconnected lifecycles. The **Nuclear Fuel Cycle** covers everything from mining uranium to managing waste. The **Power Plant Lifecycle** spans from a plant's design and construction to its final decommissioning. A company's role in the industry is defined by where it operates within these value chains.

Strategic Analysis & Outlook

The global nuclear industry is at a critical inflection point, shaped by powerful strategic forces. This section summarizes the key trends redefining corporate structures, supply chains, and technological pathways, from geopolitical shifts to the disruptive potential of new reactor designs.

Vertical Integration vs. Specialization

A key strategic battle is underway. State-owned giants like Russia's Rosatom are "vertically integrated," controlling every step from mine to reactor. This allows them to offer all-in-one deals. In response, Western companies are consolidating, like Cameco (mining) buying a stake in Westinghouse (reactors), to create a competing integrated champion. At the same time, highly specialized companies like Urenco (enrichment) or Holtec (waste storage) prove that being the best in a critical niche remains a powerful business model.

Geopolitical Realignment of Supply Chains

Geopolitics is dramatically reshaping the industry. Historic reliance on low-cost Russian nuclear fuel services is now seen as a major strategic risk. This has triggered a massive push by Western nations to "friend-shore" and build their own independent fuel supply chains. Companies like Urenco and Orano are expanding enrichment capacity in Europe and the U.S. to reduce this dependency and ensure energy security.

The Disruptive Force of Advanced Technology

Innovation is unlocking new markets. Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) are changing who buys nuclear power. Instead of just giant utilities, SMR developers like X-Energy and TerraPower are targeting industrial customers (Dow) and tech giants (Amazon, Microsoft) who need 24/7 clean power for factories and data centers. This entire future market depends on a new type of fuel: HALEU. The companies that can produce HALEU and perfect these new reactor designs will define the next era of nuclear energy.
